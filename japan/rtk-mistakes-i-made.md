Learning What Worked For Me

One thing I discovered surprisingly quickly was that you don't build the perfect study system on day one.

Or at least, I certainly didn't.

If you'd asked me at the beginning how I was going to study RTK, I'd probably have confidently told you I'd already figured it out. Looking back, that couldn't have been further from the truth. The routine stayed largely the same throughout the six months, but the way I actually studied was constantly evolving. Every few weeks I'd notice something that wasn't quite working as well as I'd hoped, make a small adjustment, and carry on. Looking back, those little adjustments slowly became my system.

The first lesson was pace.

When I started, I didn't decide I was going to learn twenty new kanji every day. I simply worked through the book chapter by chapter, which naturally worked out to around fifteen or twenty characters at a time. As I got more comfortable, I started to feel like I could push harder. I'd finish a session thinking, I could easily do another ten, so I did. Some days I learnt thirty. Some days forty. I even remember one day pushing myself to around sixty.

At first it felt fantastic. I genuinely thought I'd found a way to finish the book much faster than I'd originally planned.

The problem didn't appear until the following morning.

Every new kanji I learnt would eventually become another review card in Anki. I'd completely underestimated how quickly those reviews built up. Learning twice as many kanji didn't just mean doing twice as much work that evening, it meant creating far more work for myself over the following days and weeks. Before long, my mornings were getting longer and longer, and the excitement of learning more each day was being replaced by the dread of seeing another huge review queue.

That was probably the moment I realised something that seems obvious now.

The best pace wasn't the fastest pace.

It was the pace I could realistically sustain alongside everything else in my life.

For me, that settled at around twenty new kanji a day. Not because twenty is some magical number that everyone should follow, but because twenty was the point where I still felt challenged without creating a mountain of reviews that I'd later regret. If there's one piece of advice I'd give anyone starting RTK, it's not to copy someone else's numbers. Find the pace that works for your brain, because yours almost certainly won't be the same as mine.

The tools evolved as well.

The Anki deck I'd downloaded contained all 2,200 kanji in one enormous deck. That didn't really fit the way I was working through the book, so I reorganised it into chapters and created what Anki calls a filtered deck. That sounds far more complicated than it really is. All it meant was that Anki would only test me on the kanji I'd actually reached in the book instead of showing me cards from hundreds of pages ahead.

As the months went on, I also realised I was getting much better at recognising kanji than I was at writing them from memory. So I duplicated every card and created a second version that worked in reverse. Instead of seeing the kanji first, I'd only see the English keyword and have to write the character before checking the answer. It made my reviews take noticeably longer, but it also exposed weaknesses I never would've spotted otherwise.

Looking back, I think that's probably the biggest lesson this part of the journey taught me.

Don't spend weeks trying to build the perfect study system before you've even started.

Give yourself the right tools, start learning, and then pay attention to what your own brain is telling you. If something isn't working, change it. If something is working, keep doing it.

That's why I struggle when people online tell you there's one "correct" way to study RTK.

There isn't.

There was only ever one correct way for me.
